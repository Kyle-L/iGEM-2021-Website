  1. Fragment Amplification

    29 July 2021

    We used polymerase chain reaction (PCR) to amplify fragments that would be used for Gibson Assembly of our plasmids.

  2. Fragment Amplification

    3 August 2021

    For the overexpression plasmids -pGi-tal, pGi-fbp, and pGi-tal+fbp- we needed to amplify the transaldolase and fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase (class I) genes from the Synechococcus elongatus PCC 7942 genome.

  3. Fragment Amplification

    3 August 2021

    We took our fragments straight from the gel (3 August)

  4. Fragment Amplification

    4 August 2021

    The backbone for this plasmid was restriction digested from pAM2991, not PCR amplified (4 August)

  5. Gibson Assembly

    5 August 2021

    The fragments above have overlapping sequences with what they are next to on the plasmid. The primers we designed are what allowed for this. The overlap was used during the Gibson Assembly of our plasmids


    Transformation of E. coli

    5 August 2021

    We took our assembled plasmids and used chiical transformation to put thi in Escherichia coli dh10ùõÉ. E. coli will replicate the plasmids to give us multiple copies. S. elongatus doesn‚Äôt do this.

    Antibiotic resistance in the plasmid means we can grow cells on growth media containing antibiotics to pick out successful transformants.

  6. Purification of plasmids from E. coli

    9 August 2021

    We needed to purify our plasmids from E. coli before transforming S. elongatus.

    We broke up the plasmid (restriction digest) to make sure our genes were in there.
